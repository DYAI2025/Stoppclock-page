Most days, life doesn’t shout “Now is a good moment to talk.” Messages pop up, someone is late, dishes wait, notifications blink. It’s easy for important conversations to stay “for later”.

Section heading:
Character – a time referee for thinking games

Body copy (ca. 200–230 Wörter):

Long before digital screens, people already needed a way to keep games moving. Sand glasses gave both players a sense of time, but not much precision. Mechanical chess clocks brought something new: the idea that time is part of fairness.

In modern games, I sit between the players. I don’t care who is ahead on the board. I only care that both sides get the same chance to think. Every move you make is a tiny decision: do you use more seconds now, or save them for a tricky endgame?

I’m not here to rush you, but I also won’t let you think forever. When you see your time slowly melting away, the game changes: you look for clear ideas, not for every possible line. You learn to decide, not only to calculate.

On stoppclock, I bring this idea to your board games, your practice sessions, and even your friendly debates. If you want, I can turn any back-and-forth into a fair dance of moves and seconds.

3. Rituals – How to use me
Logisch scheint mir, drei Haupt-Rituale decken die meisten Nutzungsszenarien ab: Training, schnelle Partien, und „fair speech time“.

Section heading:
Rituals – ways to play with time

Ritual 1 – Friendly rapid game
Card title:
Friendly rapid game

Description:
Give each player a fixed pool of time so the game stays sharp but relaxed.

Steps:

Set a total time for each player (for example 10–15 minutes).

Place me next to the board where both of you can reach me.

Start me when the first move is played. After every move, tap your side so the other clock starts.

When one player’s time runs out, the game ends – you can decide together if that means a strict loss or just “time to wrap up”.

Ritual 2 – Tournament practice
Card title:
Tournament practice

Description:
Train with the kind of time control you’ll face in real events.

Steps:

Pick the time setting that matches your tournament format (for example “classic”, “rapid”, or “blitz”).

Start a practice game using that control. Take it seriously – treat your time as if it were a real event.

After the game, look back: Did you run out of time often? Did you spend too long on early moves?

Adjust and play again, trying to keep some time for complex endgames.

Ritual 3 – Fair turns in any debate
(leicht spielerische Erweiterung über Schach hinaus, aber im gleichen Charakter)

Card title:
Fair turns in a debate

Description:
Use me to keep a discussion balanced when two people have strong opinions.

Steps:

Set a total time for each side (for example 5 minutes).

Agree on a topic and a simple rule: one person speaks at a time.

Tap the clock every time you switch who is speaking.

When one side runs out of time, they listen for the rest – or you both stop and summarize what you heard.
